One of my friends had been encouraging me to read 'Hyperion' by Dan Simmons for as long as I can remember, and I recently got around to picking it up. I may do a longer review at some point, but I particularly wanted to take a closer look at the worldbuilding which I loved.

I've seen this type of worldbuilding before, but it was done so well in this book. I've spoken a bit about my experience of [[Building a world to set a campaign in|worldbuilding for a TTRPG]], but I wanted to call out what I've noticed when reading this book. I'm not sure what it would be called or if others have named it already, but I have decided to describe it as 'candid worldbuilding'.

## What is 'candid worldbuilding'?
I'm defining candid worldbuilding as the following:
> The style of worldbuilding where the reader is given none or very little context. Gaps are left intentionally empty for the reader to fill in.

In other words, 'candid worldbuilding' is the antithesis of an exposition dump.

### How to do candid worldbuilding?
**Using the real world as a reference point** - This is perhaps the most common type of candid worldbuilding I noticed in Hyperion, which is set (no spoilers) several hundred years in our future. This gives it the unique opportunity to utilise the readers existing knowledge of the world and how it works to subtly expand the world, without needing to descibe everything exactly. Dan Simmons does this regularly through the use of listing several _things_, which includes some from the real world, and some from the book world.

A great example of this is found in **the poets tale**, where the following is said:


**Trust the reader to fill in the gaps** - _make up names of cities/planets etc._
